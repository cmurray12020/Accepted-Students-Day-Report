01 · EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
The headline

Accepted Students Day is still one of the biggest levers we have.

Nine in ten attendees called the day worthwhile and left more excited about becoming a Golden Knight. Nearly half told us Clarkson is now the only school they're considering. The experience is landing, and it's doing the heavy lifting admissions needs it to do.

"[On what could have been done differently:] No, it's the best accepted students day of any college out there."

But the signal in the data isn't all gold. Communications slipped year over year, lunch logistics created real friction, and non-engineering students and Clarkson School attendees told us they felt like an afterthought. These are fixable problems, and fixing them is where next year's gains live.

TL;DR for leadership

Five things to know.

  • WIN91% rate the day "worthwhile." Excitement and decision influence both land above 75%.
  • WIN"Only school being considered" jumped from 42% pre-event to 65% post-event. ASD is actively closing the decision.
  • WINStaff, student, and faculty friendliness scored 95% favorable. People are the product.
  • WATCHPre-event communications dropped from 95% favorable (2025) to 75% (2026). Neutral responses tripled.
  • FIXLunch is the single most-cited pain point. Food ran out, timing overlapped the showcase, and logistics felt scattered.
  • FIXClarkson School students and non-engineering majors told us programming felt one-size-fits-all.

Reading the signal

The three core sentiment questions dipped only slightly and remain strong. A 2–5 point movement on a sample this size is within the margin of normal variation.

Communications is a different story. Neutral responses jumped from 2 last year to 10 this year, and we picked up a "Highly Unfavorable" we didn't see in 2025. Open-ended feedback corroborates it: students cited unanswered emails about overnight stays, last-minute schedule delivery, and missing appointment confirmations.

"I sent letters to emails that were given on emails and website but never received any answer. It would be cool if communication was better."

01

Current students carry the day.

Nearly every positive write-in mentioned talking with current Clarkson students. Their honesty and accessibility are doing more to convert admits than any piece of marketing we ship.

"Meeting current students and hearing their honest experiences."
02

The split-by-major format works.

Program-specific sessions, mock classes, and meeting with the department of interest came up repeatedly as the single most valuable hour of the day. It's what turns a generic visit into a specific decision.

"Getting split into the different majors to learn and experience more of what it would be like in what I actually want to study."
03

Professors close the deal.

Multiple responses cited one-on-one professor conversations as the pivot point. Attendees specifically named David Schelly, the aerospace engineering presentation, and the Business School mock class as standouts.

"When we broke off into our separate groups, the person explaining my major was actually a professor. I liked that I got to see what a real professor at Clarkson is like."
04

The overnight program is a hidden weapon.

Only a handful of students participated, but those who did called it "100% my favorite part" and credited it with amplifying their excitement. Worth scaling if capacity allows.

"I did the overnight option and it was 100% my favorite part, and it majorly amplified my excitement and experience at Clarkson."

What worked

Attendees pointed to the alumni and current student panels, pre-planned panel questions, and conversations with corporate partners as the most valuable components. One student specifically called out the value of "creating a picture of what my life could look like in ten or twenty years."

"Connecting with alumni and future students."

For the three overnight stay participants, experiences were largely positive. Student hosts rated "Extremely Helpful" by two of three respondents, and comfort levels ran from "Somewhat Comfortable" to "Very Comfortable." One student described the overnight as the "deciding factor" in a one-word summary of their experience.

What needs work

The strongest critique echoes the main ASD finding: programming felt engineering-heavy. Both the parent respondent and a business-major student independently flagged this as the biggest gap.

"The theme of the day seemed to be about engineering. My daughter is going to study business, it would have been nice if other programs were also represented."

Other suggestions: better explanation of networking games, more one-on-one time with corporate partners, more student involvement, and an additional meal swipe for overnight guests.

09 · AREAS FOR IMPROVEMENT
Themes from "Anything done differently?"

Five areas to focus on for next year.

Across 55 open-ended responses, these five themes appeared repeatedly. None require a budget overhaul. All are operational.

01

Lunch logistics need a rethink.

By a wide margin, lunch is the most-cited frustration. Food ran out. Plates ran out. Timing overlapped with the Showcase, which meant students had to choose between eating and meeting clubs. Some clubs were packing up by the time late eaters finished.

"Food ran out multiple times, and so did plates. By the time I got food, some of the clubs were packing up."
"Better lunch, there wasn't enough food for everyone."
Separate lunch and Showcase windows, or increase food volume and staffing for simultaneous flow. Consider using the student cafeterias, one attendee asked for it by name.
02

Non-engineering students need more visibility in general programming.

Multiple respondents, including one who ultimately chose not to apply to the Honors College because of it, said student panels and information felt engineering-dominant. Business students in particular felt underrepresented until they reached their own program session.

"Make sure to include other majors besides just engineering to capture all of Clarkson. I struggled with getting a feel for the business students/school."
Diversify panelist majors in general sessions. Make sure business, health sciences, and humanities majors are visible in the Welcome and Beyond the Classroom panels, not only in their own silos.
03

The Clarkson School needs dedicated programming.

Two separate TCS attendees flagged feeling "out of place" and asked for programming specifically tailored to their unique status. Given TCS is a real competitive differentiator, generic programming is a missed opportunity.

"More for TCS students to do, not just generalized to the whole college. I felt very out of place since I wasn't just a normal first year student."
Build a small TCS-dedicated track or clearly flag existing TCS-specific moments in the day's schedule. The TCS Showcase was mentioned as the thing that sealed the deal for one attendee.
04

Pre-event communications need to arrive earlier and with clearer ownership.

This is the 20-point YoY drop in communication favorability made concrete. Students asked for the schedule in advance (not day-of), clearer overnight stay logistics, and responses to pre-event emails they sent about financial aid and overnight registration.

"Being sent the schedule via email or text in advance could have been more helpful than receiving it the day of."
"I wish there was better communication about sleeping arrangements."
Audit the pre-event comms calendar: confirm the schedule lands at least 72 hours out, and assign clear ownership for responding to the inboxes listed in confirmation emails.
05

The Engineering Showcase space is too crowded.

Snell Hall was called out as loud and crowded, making it hard to hear at stations or reach every club. One attendee was specifically disappointed they couldn't find the club (lacrosse) they came for.

"The engineering showcase was very crowded which made it difficult to get to all of the stations."
"I wish more clubs attended the showcase."
Consider spreading the Showcase across two adjacent spaces or staggering attendance windows. Audit club RSVP list against what actually showed.
10 · RECOMMENDATIONS
Where to invest for 2027

Priority areas for the next ASD cycle.

Based on what the 2026 cohort told us, here's where focus should land for the next Accepted Students Day.

A

Overhaul lunch logistics.

Highest-frequency complaint, lowest-cost fix. Decouple lunch and Showcase timing, add capacity, and select a venue students can find easily.

B

Rebuild the pre-event communications sequence.

Recover the 20-point communication drop. Send schedules earlier, assign response ownership, and close the loop on overnight and appointment requests.

C

Build non-engineering visibility into the main stage.

Program the Welcome and Beyond the Classroom panels with intentional major diversity. Business, Health Sciences, and Humanities students should see themselves in the opening hour.

D

Scale what's already winning: students and professors.

The strongest converter in the data is time with Clarkson humans. Protect and expand program-session time and current-student access.

E

Pilot dedicated TCS programming.

Small group, high conversion. A two-hour afternoon track just for Clarkson School admits could turn TCS from footnote into flagship experience.

11 · METHODOLOGY & NOTES

About the data

Primary ASD data pulled from Slate Quick Query on April 13, 2026. Primary cohort: 57 responses with a 2026 submission date. YoY comparisons use 2025's 61-response cohort. Corporate Leadership Day data pulled April 14, 2026 with 5 responses.

Percentages in logistics scorecards reflect positive sentiment ("Highly Favorable" or "Slightly Favorable") as a share of all responses to that question. Decision-factor mentions combine primary and secondary reasons, so totals exceed respondent count by design.

Caveats worth stating

Survey respondents are self-selected from those who attended. This dataset cannot speak for no-shows, no-responses, or students who chose a different school. The sentiment reported here reflects the experience of engaged admits, not the broader admitted pool.
